# 04 — Claim Assessment Templates (initial checklist structures)

These are the seed templates for MVP. Every template shares a common spine, then adds
claim-type-specific sections. Each checklist item carries: prompt text, answer type,
evidence-required flag, red-flag indicator, and notes. Templates must be reviewed by
working assessors before Phase 2 build — **the templates are the product**.

## 4.0 Common spine (all claim types)

**Opening (every session):**
- Confirm identity of the person on camera (full name, relationship to policyholder).
- Confirm consent was accepted; confirm no one else is being filmed without awareness.
- Confirm loss address matches the risk address on the policy — ask the client to show
  something that anchors location where practical (street view from window/gate,
  utility bill on camera).
- Date of loss, how it was discovered, who discovered it.
- Has any emergency mitigation been done? By whom? Invoices kept?
- Any prior claims for the same/similar damage? Any prior unrepaired damage?

**Closing (every session):**
- Panoramic capture of the affected room/area (context shots — never only close-ups).
- Client confirms all damage claimed has been shown.
- Explain next steps and the missing-evidence follow-up if applicable.

**Universal red flags (available on every item):** inconsistent account vs. physical
evidence; damage older than claimed; claim shortly after inception or cover increase;
reluctance/inability to show requested areas; over-rehearsed narrative; third party
"managing" the client off-camera.

## 4.1 Geyser / water damage

*(Highest-volume SA household claim — this template must be excellent.)*

**Evidence to capture**
- Geyser unit wide shot (location: roof cavity / cupboard / external).
- **Rating plate close-up** — make, model, serial number, capacity, manufacture date.
- Point of failure (burst seam, element flange, fitting) if visible.
- Drip tray and overflow pipe — presence, routing to exterior, condition.
- Pressure control valve / safety valve and vacuum breakers.
- Isolation: electrical isolator switch, water shut-off.
- Water damage path: ceiling boards (stains, sagging, collapse), walls, cornices.
- Floor coverings and affected contents, item by item.
- Any completed emergency repairs / replacement geyser installed (plus new unit's plate).

**Questions the assessor must answer**
- Age of the geyser? (plate date, invoice, or client estimate — record source.)
- Sudden burst or gradual leak? How long was water visibly present before action?
- Was the installation compliant (drip tray, overflow, valves per SANS 10254)?
- Who did the emergency work — insurer-appointed plumber or client's own?
- Is the resultant damage consistent with the described failure and time frame?
- Is the geyser itself covered, resultant damage, or both, per the policy?

**Common red flags**
- Long-term staining/mould indicating a slow leak claimed as sudden burst
  (wear-and-tear / gradual deterioration exclusion).
- Rating plate date shows geyser older than client states; element failure claimed as burst.
- Non-compliant installation (no drip tray/overflow) contributing to resultant damage.
- Resultant damage claimed in rooms implausibly far from the geyser.
- Replacement already done at inflated cost with no old unit retained for inspection.

**Required documents**
- Plumber's report and invoice (emergency + replacement).
- Purchase/installation invoice or certificate of compliance for the geyser if available.
- Quotes for resultant damage repairs (ceiling, paint, flooring).
- Contents list with replacement quotes/proof for damaged items.

**Report wording considerations**
- Distinguish clearly: *geyser unit*, *resultant water damage*, *emergency mitigation costs*.
- State the evidence basis for geyser age and failure mode ("rating plate observed on
  video, manufactured 2019-03" vs "insured's estimate — plate inaccessible").
- If installation non-compliance observed, state facts observed, not legal conclusions.
- Note any damage attributed to gradual deterioration separately from the sudden event.

## 4.2 Storm damage

**Evidence to capture**
- Exterior elevations of the building (all affected sides, wide shots).
- Roof damage as viewable — from ground, window, or client-held camera at a safe
  vantage point (**never instruct the client onto the roof or a ladder** — standing
  safety instruction shown in-session and logged).
- Displaced/broken tiles or sheeting, flashing, ridge capping.
- Gutters and downpipes — damage AND pre-existing condition (rust, sagging, blockage).
- Water ingress points inside: ceilings, walls, around windows/doors.
- Fences, walls, carports, outbuildings, paving, pool equipment if claimed.
- Fallen trees/branches and what they struck.
- Affected contents, item by item.

**Questions the assessor must answer**
- Date/time of the storm event; does it match known weather activity for the area?
  (Assessor corroborates via weather records — note the source in the report.)
- Is the damage pattern consistent with wind/hail/rain of the reported event?
- What was the pre-storm condition of the damaged elements (maintenance state)?
- Single event or accumulation of prior damage?
- Any temporary repairs (tarpaulins etc.) — reasonable and invoiced?

**Common red flags**
- Rusted gutters, perished waterproofing, rotten fascias claimed as storm damage
  (maintenance/wear exclusion).
- Damage on elevations sheltered from the reported wind direction.
- General dilapidation with one storm event used to claim full refurbishment.
- Internal water stains with old paint bubbling/mould (long-standing ingress).
- No corroborating weather event on the reported date.

**Required documents**
- Repair quotes (roof, ceilings, painting, fencing).
- Invoices for emergency/temporary repairs.
- Contents replacement quotes where applicable.
- Photos taken by the client at the time of the event, if any (request originals with
  metadata via upload link, not WhatsApp re-compressed copies where possible).

**Report wording considerations**
- Separate storm-attributable damage from maintenance-related deterioration explicitly,
  item by item — this is the core of most storm claim disputes.
- Record the weather corroboration source and finding.
- Note the vantage limitations of a virtual roof inspection and recommend physical/
  drone inspection where the roof is material to quantum and not adequately viewable.

## 4.3 Theft / burglary

**Evidence to capture**
- **Point of entry** — close-up and context shots: damaged door/lock/window/burglar bars,
  tool marks, broken glass position (inside vs outside).
- All other potential entry points (to confirm secured/undamaged).
- Security measures in place: burglar bars, security gates, alarm panel (show armed
  status/keypad), beams, electric fence energizer, CCTV equipment.
- Alarm panel/keypad close-up; ask client to show alarm company signage/contract.
- Locations items were taken from (empty TV bracket, jewellery drawer, safe).
- The safe itself if jewellery/firearms claimed (type, mounting, damage).
- Remaining similar items (supports lifestyle consistency).
- General interior condition (ransacking vs. targeted removal).

**Questions the assessor must answer**
- SAPS case number obtained? Station? Date reported? (Number on camera or via upload.)
- Forcible and violent entry evident, consistent with policy requirements?
- Were policy security conditions met (alarm armed and monitored, burglar bars fitted,
  safe used for specified jewellery/firearms)?
- Who was home / where was the insured; timeline of discovery.
- Alarm activation history — did the armed response attend? (Request alarm company
  event report.)
- Itemised list of stolen goods with proof of ownership per significant item.

**Common red flags**
- No signs of forced entry ("mysterious disappearance" vs theft cover).
- Glass broken from the inside; tool marks inconsistent with described entry.
- Alarm not armed / monitoring lapsed where policy requires it.
- High-value items recently added to the policy; claim soon after inception.
- Ownership proof absent for most items; items inconsistent with visible lifestyle.
- Serial numbers unknown for all electronics; receipts all from the same source/date.
- Prior similar theft claims.

**Required documents**
- SAPS case number confirmation / police report extract.
- Alarm company activation/attendance report (where applicable).
- Proof of ownership: receipts, bank/card statements, photos showing items in situ,
  valuation certificates (jewellery), ICASA/serial records for electronics.
- Itemised claimed list with replacement quotes.

**Report wording considerations**
- Describe entry evidence factually with photo references; avoid stating "staged" —
  record observations and inconsistencies, and recommend further investigation where
  warranted.
- Address each policy security warranty explicitly: requirement → observed status →
  evidence reference.
- List ownership verification status per major item (verified / partially / unverified).

## 4.4 Fire damage

**Evidence to capture**
- Exterior context shots of the property and the affected structure.
- **Apparent area of origin** — widest to closest shots; burn/smoke patterns
  (V-patterns, depth of char) as visible on camera.
- Electrical distribution board and any suspect appliances/wiring at the origin.
- Room-by-room damage: structure, ceilings, contents; smoke/soot spread; heat damage
  vs. smoke-only areas.
- Fire brigade attendance evidence (report number if available).
- Salvageable vs destroyed contents; ask client to show remains of claimed items
  (appliance carcasses, bed frames, etc.).
- Utilities status: was electricity connected/prepaid active; gas installations.

**Questions the assessor must answer**
- Where did the fire start and what is the most probable cause per available evidence?
- Who was present; how was it discovered; response timeline.
- Did the fire brigade attend? Report obtainable?
- Is the damage distribution consistent with the described origin and timeline?
- Occupancy status at the time (occupied/unoccupied/vacant — policy implications).
- Extent: structural vs contents vs smoke damage; is the property habitable
  (alternative accommodation trigger)?

**Common red flags**
- Multiple apparent origin points; unusually rapid or intense burn for the fuel load.
- Absence of normal household contents in the debris (removed before the fire).
- Financial distress indicators volunteered/known; property recently marketed unsold.
- Recent increase in sum insured; cover recently incepted.
- Conflicting accounts of discovery/timeline between household members.
- Vacant/unoccupied property with unexplained fire.

**Required documents**
- Fire brigade report (where attended).
- SAPS case number if opened (arson suspicion or as required).
- Electrician's report where electrical cause suspected.
- Contents inventory with proof of ownership for significant items.
- Structural repair quotes / quantity surveyor input for major damage.

**Report wording considerations**
- A virtual assessment CANNOT determine fire cause definitively. The template report
  must state scope limitations and, for anything beyond minor/contained fires,
  recommend physical forensic investigation where cause or quantum is material.
- Record observations neutrally; flag indicators, never allege arson.
- Address habitability and mitigation (securing the property) explicitly.

## 4.5 Accidental damage

**Evidence to capture**
- The damaged item: overall shots + close-ups of the damage from multiple angles.
- Serial/model plate of the item where applicable.
- The location where the incident happened, with the client walking through what occurred.
- Any related damage (e.g. the tile the TV fell onto, liquid residue near electronics).
- Proof the item was owned/functional (photos in situ, the item powering on if partially
  functional).

**Questions the assessor must answer**
- Exact circumstances: what happened, who was involved, when.
- Is the damage physically consistent with the described mechanism?
  (Impact point, crack propagation, liquid ingress pattern.)
- Is it accidental damage or wear-and-tear/defect/gradual damage?
- Repairable vs. beyond economic repair? Repair quote obtained?
- Correct section of cover (specified all-risks item vs. household contents accidental
  damage extension)?

**Common red flags**
- Damage pattern inconsistent with the account (e.g. pressure crack on a "dropped" screen).
- Item at/near end of life; failure presented as accident.
- Repeat accidental damage claims on similar items.
- No proof of ownership for a recently "damaged" high-value item.
- Damage to an item conveniently just after upgrade desire (older phone/TV models).

**Required documents**
- Repair quote or beyond-economic-repair (BER) letter from an approved repairer.
- Purchase proof for the item (receipt, statement, packaging with serial).
- Specified-items schedule extract where relevant.

**Report wording considerations**
- Describe the damage mechanism assessment explicitly: account given vs. physical
  indicators observed.
- State repair-vs-replace recommendation with the quantum basis (quote reference).
- Keep proportionate — this template should be the shortest; don't burden small claims
  with fire-claim-level process.

## 4.6 General non-motor claim (catch-all)

For claim types without a dedicated template (impact damage, power surge, burst pipes
other than geyser, malicious damage, glass, etc.). Structure:

**Evidence to capture**
- Context shots of the property/area; close-ups of all claimed damage/items.
- Cause-related evidence appropriate to the peril (e.g. for power surge: DB board,
  surge protection presence, all affected appliances + rating plates; for impact:
  the impacting object/vehicle evidence; for burst pipe: the pipe section and access
  point).
- Documents shown on camera or flagged for upload.

**Questions the assessor must answer**
- Peril identification: what actually caused the loss, and is it an insured peril?
- Date of loss and discovery; consistency of account with evidence.
- Pre-existing condition/maintenance contribution?
- Full extent of damage; quantum basis (quotes/invoices).
- Any policy conditions/warranties relevant to this peril, and compliance status.

**Common red flags**
- Cause vague or shifting between tellings.
- Damage inconsistent with the named peril.
- Accumulated damage claimed as a single event.
- Quantum documents all sourced after the fact from a single provider at round numbers.

**Required documents**
- Cause-supporting documents (electrician/plumber/technician report as applicable).
- Repair/replacement quotes.
- Proof of ownership for items.
- Any third-party details (impact by vehicle: driver/insurer details, SAPS accident ref).

**Report wording considerations**
- Because there is no specialised structure, the assessor's narrative matters more:
  the report must include an explicit "cause of loss" finding with evidence basis, and
  a policy-relevance comment (peril match, exclusions observed) without making the
  claims decision.

## 4.7 Template mechanics (applies to all)

- Item answer types: yes/no, single choice, multi choice, short text, long text,
  number, date, evidence-only.
- Every item has: `evidence_required` (bool), `min_captures` (int), `allow_skip`
  (bool, reason mandatory), `red_flag_capable` (bool).
- Sections are ordered but the assessor can jump freely — real conversations don't
  follow scripts; the checklist tracks completeness, not sequence.
- Skipped/missing items must appear in the report's "Limitations & outstanding items"
  section automatically. Nothing silently disappears.
